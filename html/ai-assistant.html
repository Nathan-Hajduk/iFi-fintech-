How much money do I need to start investing?

You can start with as little as $50-$100. Many brokers now offer fractional shares, allowing you to invest in expensive stocks with small amounts.

What's the difference between investing and trading?

Investing is long-term wealth building (years), while trading involves frequent buying/selling for short-term gains. Investing is generally safer for beginners.

How do I choose my first investment?

Start with broad-market index funds or ETFs like S&P 500. They provide instant diversification and lower risk than individual stocks.

What's dollar-cost averaging?

Investing a fixed amount regularly (e.g., $200/month) regardless of market conditions. This reduces timing risk and builds wealth systematically.

Should I pay off debt before investing?

Pay off high-interest debt (>7-8%) first. For lower-interest debt, you can often invest and pay debt simultaneously for better overall returns.

What's a diversified portfolio?

Spreading investments across different assets (stocks, bonds, real estate) and sectors to reduce risk. Don't put all eggs in one basket.

What exactly is a stock?

A stock represents partial ownership in a company. When you buy shares, you own a piece of that business and can profit from its growth.

How do I research a stock before buying?

Look at P/E ratio, revenue growth, profit margins, debt levels, and competitive advantages. Read earnings reports and industry news.

What are dividends?

Regular cash payments companies make to shareholders from profits. Dividend stocks provide passive income in addition to potential price appreciation.

Growth stocks vs. value stocks?

Growth stocks have high potential but higher risk (tech startups). Value stocks are established companies trading below their intrinsic value.

When should I sell a stock?

When fundamentals change (poor earnings, management issues), if you need to rebalance, or if better opportunities emerge. Don't panic-sell during dips.

What's a stock market index?

A collection of stocks representing a market segment. S&P 500 tracks 500 large US companies. Indexes are benchmarks for market performance.

What are bonds and how do they work?

Bonds are loans you give to governments or corporations. They pay you interest regularly and return your principal at maturity. Lower risk than stocks.

Government bonds vs. corporate bonds?

Government bonds (Treasury) are safest but lower returns. Corporate bonds pay more but have higher default risk. Rating agencies assess credit quality.

Why invest in bonds?

Bonds provide stable income, reduce portfolio volatility, and preserve capital. Essential for diversification and risk management, especially near retirement.

What's a bond ladder?

Buying bonds with staggered maturity dates (e.g., 1, 3, 5, 7 years). Provides regular access to cash while earning interest across the ladder.

How do interest rates affect bonds?

When rates rise, existing bond prices fall (and vice versa). If you hold until maturity, rate changes don't affect your return.

What are municipal bonds?

Bonds issued by state/local governments. Interest is often tax-free, making them attractive for high-income investors.

What's a 401(k)?

Employer-sponsored retirement account. Contributions are pre-tax, reducing your current tax bill. Many employers match contributions—free money!

Traditional IRA vs. Roth IRA?

Traditional: tax deduction now, pay taxes on withdrawals. Roth: pay taxes now, tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Depends on your current vs. future tax bracket.

How much should I save for retirement?

Rule of thumb: save 15% of income. Aim to replace 70-80% of pre-retirement income. Use retirement calculators to estimate your needs.

What's the 4% rule?

Withdraw 4% of your retirement portfolio annually for sustainable income. Based on historical data suggesting portfolios last 30+ years.

Should I max out my 401(k)?

At minimum, contribute enough to get full employer match. After that, max Roth IRA, then return to 401(k) if you can save more.

What's Social Security?

Government retirement benefit based on your earnings history. You can claim at 62 (reduced) or wait until 70 (maximum benefit).

What is cryptocurrency?

Digital currency using blockchain technology. Bitcoin was first; now thousands exist. Decentralized, no government or bank control.

Is crypto a good investment?

Extremely volatile and speculative. Can offer high returns but also huge losses. Only invest what you can afford to lose—typically 5% or less of portfolio.

Bitcoin vs. Ethereum vs. others?

Bitcoin: digital gold, store of value. Ethereum: smart contracts platform. Altcoins: various use cases but most are highly risky.

How do I buy cryptocurrency?

Use exchanges like Coinbase, Kraken, or Binance. Store in exchange wallet (convenient) or hardware wallet (more secure for large amounts).

What's blockchain?

Distributed ledger technology that records all transactions across a network. Transparent, immutable, and decentralized.

Are there crypto taxes?

Yes! Crypto sales are taxable events. Track all transactions for capital gains reporting. Rules vary by country—consult a tax professional.

Should I invest in real estate?

Real estate provides rental income, tax benefits, and appreciation potential. Requires more capital and effort than stocks but can diversify portfolio.

What are REITs?

Real Estate Investment Trusts—companies that own/operate income-producing properties. Invest in real estate without buying property directly.

Buying rental property: good idea?

Can generate passive income and build equity. Consider mortgage, maintenance, vacancies, and property management. Aim for positive cash flow.

How much down payment do I need?

Primary residence: 3-20%. Investment property: typically 20-25%. Higher down payment means lower monthly costs and better terms.

What's house flipping?

Buy undervalued property, renovate, sell for profit. Requires capital, renovation skills, and market knowledge. Higher risk than buy-and-hold.

Real estate vs. stock market?

Real estate: tangible, leverage via mortgages, tax benefits. Stocks: more liquid, easier diversification, lower entry cost. Both have merit.
